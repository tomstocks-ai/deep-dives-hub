## 1. Company Overview

Bloom Energy Corporation (NYSE: BE) designs, manufactures, and sells solid-oxide fuel cell systems for on-site power generation. Its flagship product, the Bloom Energy Server, converts natural gas or biogas into electricity through an electrochemical process without combustion, delivering reliable baseload power with lower carbon emissions than traditional grid power.

**Business Model:** Bloom operates a hardware-plus-service model. Customers purchase or lease Energy Servers, and Bloom signs long-term service agreements (typically 10–20 years) for maintenance, fuel procurement, and performance guarantees. This creates a recurring revenue stream alongside upfront product sales.

**Revenue Segments:**
- **Product Revenue:** Sale and installation of Energy Servers (~87% of Q1 2026 revenue)
- **Service Revenue:** Long-term service agreements, maintenance, and fuel logistics (~13% of Q1 2026 revenue)

**Competitive Moat:** Bloom is the only scaled U.S. manufacturer of solid-oxide fuel cells (SOFC) for commercial and industrial applications. The technology operates at high efficiency (~60% electrical efficiency) and can run on hydrogen, natural gas, or biogas — a flexibility advantage over proton-exchange membrane (PEM) competitors. The company holds a deep patent portfolio and has over two decades of operational data across thousands of systems.

**Key Partnerships:** Expanded 2.8 GW master agreement with Oracle for AI data center power, and a \$5 billion partnership with Brookfield Asset Management for data center infrastructure. These validate commercial demand at scale.

**Management:** CEO K. R. Sridhar founded the company in 2001 and has steered it from NASA research origins through IPO (2018) to commercial scale. The leadership team has deep engineering and energy infrastructure experience.

## 4. Growth Catalysts

1. **Data Center Electrification Boom:** AI data centers require massive, reliable baseload power. Bloom's 2.8 GW Oracle deal and \$5B Brookfield partnership position it as a primary beneficiary. Data center power demand is expected to grow 2–3x by 2030.

2. **Hydrogen Readiness:** Bloom Energy Servers can run on 100% hydrogen. As green hydrogen scales, Bloom can pivot customers to zero-carbon fuel without hardware replacement — a significant long-term optionality.

3. **Backlog Conversion:** \$20B backlog (\$6B product + \$14B service) provides 5+ years of revenue visibility. The service backlog is particularly valuable — high-margin, recurring, and long-duration.

4. **Margin Expansion:** Gross margin target of ~34% for FY2026, up from historical levels in the mid-20s. Operating leverage from scale and manufacturing learning curve should drive further expansion in 2027+.

5. **International Expansion:** Currently U.S.-focused with early international pilots. Europe and Asia represent large addressable markets with aggressive decarbonization targets.

6. **Policy Tailwinds:** IRA tax credits, state-level clean energy mandates, and potential federal data center power infrastructure investments all benefit Bloom's value proposition.

## 5. Risk Factors

**Business Risks:**
- **Customer Concentration:** Large deals (Oracle, Brookfield) represent significant revenue concentration. Loss or delay of a major contract would materially impact guidance.
- **Execution Risk:** Scaling manufacturing to meet \$3.8B revenue guidance requires flawless supply chain and production ramp. Any bottleneck hits revenue and margins.
- **Technology Risk:** SOFC is a proven technology, but PEM fuel cells and battery storage are competing solutions. If batteries or PEM achieve cost parity for baseload applications, Bloom's advantage erodes.
- **Fuel Price Exposure:** Natural gas price volatility affects customer economics and service contract profitability.

**Financial Risks:**
- **Valuation Risk:** Trading at ~11x forward sales with a ~\$41B market cap. Any growth deceleration or margin miss will trigger severe multiple compression.
- **Cash Burn:** While \$2.5B cash is substantial, rapid scaling may require additional capex. Monitor cash flow trends closely.
- **Debt:** Historical debt levels have been manageable, but any new borrowing for capacity expansion adds leverage.

**Macro Risks:**
- **Interest Rates:** High-growth, cash-burning (historically) stocks are sensitive to rate environments. Rising rates compress growth multiples.
- **Regulatory:** Changes to clean energy subsidies or data center permitting could slow demand.
- **Competition:** General Electric, Siemens, and emerging fuel cell startups are all targeting the data center power market.

## 7. Readability Pass

**Fuel Cell:** A device that converts chemical energy from fuel (natural gas, hydrogen, biogas) into electricity through an electrochemical reaction — like a battery that never runs out as long as fuel is supplied.

**Solid-Oxide Fuel Cell (SOFC):** A type of fuel cell that operates at very high temperatures (~800°C). This high temperature allows it to use a variety of fuels and achieve high electrical efficiency, but it also means the materials must be extremely durable.

**Baseload Power:** The minimum level of demand on an electrical grid over a span of time. Baseload power sources are those that run continuously (like coal, nuclear, or Bloom's fuel cells) as opposed to intermittent sources (like wind or solar).

**Backlog:** The total value of orders and contracts that have been signed but not yet delivered or recognized as revenue. Bloom's \$20B backlog means customers have already committed to buying \$20B worth of products and services.

**Non-GAAP:** Financial metrics that exclude certain accounting charges (like stock-based compensation or amortization) to show what the business looks like operationally. Always compare non-GAAP to GAAP to understand the full picture.

**Adjusted EBITDA:** Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization, adjusted for one-time items. A proxy for cash-generating ability of the core business.
