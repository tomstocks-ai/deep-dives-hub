# NOVT — Novanta Inc.

Novanta is a precision-technology enabler — the picks-and-shovels inside surgical robots, DNA sequencers, semiconductor tools, and factory automation. It supplies photonics (laser beam steering and sources), precision motion, and machine-vision subsystems to medical and advanced-industrial OEMs. FY2025 revenue rose 3% to \$981M with adjusted EPS of \$3.29, and momentum is re-accelerating: Q1 2026 bookings grew 37% (a 1.10 book-to-bill), and management raised FY2026 guidance. The \$1.45B Riverpoint Medical acquisition pushes Novanta deeper into recurring surgical consumables. The catch is valuation and risk: at ~\$149 the stock trades ~40x forward (and ~105x depressed GAAP) earnings, and the debt-and-equity-funded deal adds leverage, dilution, and integration risk. High-quality compounder, full price — a **HOLD**.

> **Classification note:** Novanta straddles two themes. Medical *end markets* are ~53% of sales, but its larger *reporting segment* is Automation Enabling Technologies (~51%), and its core identity is precision/photonics/motion enabling technology for robotics and automation (it is a holding in the ROBO robotics-and-automation index). We classify it under **AI Buildout → Robotics & Automation** on that basis, while flagging the substantial and growing medical exposure below.

## 1. Company Overview

**Novanta Inc.** (NASDAQ: NOVT), headquartered in Boston, Massachusetts, is a trusted technology partner to leading global medical and industrial original equipment manufacturers (OEMs). Founded in 1968 (formerly GSI Group), Novanta designs and manufactures the high-precision photonics, motion, and vision subsystems that go *inside* its customers' equipment — from robotic surgery systems and genomics instruments to semiconductor and electronics manufacturing tools.

### Business Model and Revenue Segments

Novanta reports in two segments, and also frames its exposure by end market:

| View | Split | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Automation Enabling Technologies** (segment) | ~51% of revenue | Laser beam steering and scanning, laser sources, precision/robotic motion, end-of-arm tooling, bearing spindles, machine vision |
| **Medical Solutions** (segment) | ~49% of revenue | Intelligent medical subsystems, minimally invasive and advanced-surgery components, and (post-Riverpoint) surgical consumables |
| **Medical end markets** | ~53% of sales | Some medical revenue sits within Automation Enabling Technologies |
| **Advanced industrial end markets** | ~47% of sales | Semiconductor, electronics, scientific instrumentation, industrial automation |

The strategic thrust is toward **recurring revenue** and **intelligent subsystems**: medical consumables already represent ~15% of sales and grow at a double-digit rate, and the Riverpoint Medical acquisition materially expands that recurring, single-use surgical-consumables base.

### Competitive Moat

- **Designed-in, mission-critical components:** Novanta's subsystems are engineered into customers' platforms over multi-year design cycles. Once specified into a surgical robot or a sequencer, they are extremely sticky and carry long lifecycles plus consumable/service pull-through.
- **Precision technology breadth:** A rare combination of photonics, precision motion, and vision under one roof lets Novanta sell integrated subsystems rather than commodity parts.
- **Recurring revenue shift:** Growing medical consumables and intelligent subsystems improve revenue durability and margins.
- **Serial-acquirer playbook:** A disciplined M&A engine (most recently Riverpoint) adds capabilities and recurring revenue, compounding the portfolio over time.

### Management

CEO **Matthijs Glastra** leads a management team that has reshaped Novanta toward higher-growth medical and automation markets, reduced semiconductor cyclicality, and prioritized recurring revenue. CFO **Robert Buckley** is a frequent presence on the investor circuit. Management pairs organic innovation with a steady acquisition cadence and has guided to double-digit growth over time through that combined strategy.

## 4. Growth Catalysts

1. **Riverpoint Medical integration:** The \$1.45B acquisition of a minimally invasive surgical-consumables maker is immediately accretive, expands high-margin recurring revenue, and leverages Novanta's existing medical OEM relationships. Clean execution is the single biggest value driver.

2. **Bookings re-acceleration:** Q1 2026 bookings grew 37% YoY with a 1.10 book-to-bill across all businesses — a leading indicator that revenue growth is inflecting higher after a soft patch.

3. **Recurring medical consumables:** Already ~15% of sales and growing double digits; Riverpoint materially increases this durable, single-use revenue base and smooths the historically capital-equipment-driven cycle.

4. **Automation and robotics demand:** The Automation Enabling Technologies segment supplies precision motion, laser, and machine-vision subsystems into robotics, semiconductor, and lab-automation systems — with management citing AI-linked and GenAI-adjacent technology demand.

5. **Serial-acquirer compounding:** A disciplined M&A pipeline plus organic innovation targets sustained double-digit growth; further bolt-ons could extend the recurring-revenue mix.

## 5. Risk Factors

### Business Risks

- **Integration and M&A risk:** Riverpoint is Novanta's largest deal in years. Integration missteps, culture/systems friction, or overpayment would directly hit the thesis.
- **Cyclicality:** Advanced-industrial and semiconductor end markets (~47% of sales) are cyclical; a capex downturn slows bookings, as Novanta has experienced before.
- **OEM concentration and dependence:** As a component/subsystem supplier, Novanta's growth depends on its OEM customers' own product cycles and success.

### Financial Risks

- **Full valuation:** At ~40x forward adjusted (and ~105x trailing GAAP) earnings, the stock embeds continued execution; multiple compression is the main downside driver.
- **Leverage and dilution:** The debt-plus-\$300M-equity funding of Riverpoint raises net debt and share count, pressuring per-share metrics until synergies and deleveraging arrive.
- **GAAP earnings optics:** Heavy acquisition amortization keeps GAAP EPS low and the headline P/E extreme, which can weigh on sentiment.

### Macro and Sector Risks

- **Tariffs and supply chain:** Novanta has flagged tariff and cost headwinds; global manufacturing exposure adds FX and supply risk.
- **Small/mid-cap volatility:** A ~1.7 beta and mid-cap liquidity make the stock sensitive to risk-off rotations.
- **Healthcare and industrial demand cycles:** Softness in either primary end market would slow the combined growth rate.

## 6. Recommendation

**Rating: <span class="rating-hold">HOLD</span>**

Novanta is a high-quality precision-technology compounder with a genuine moat: mission-critical photonics, motion, and vision subsystems designed into medical and industrial platforms, an accelerating bookings trend (+37% in Q1 2026), a growing recurring-consumables mix, and a disciplined M&A engine now extended by the \$1.45B Riverpoint acquisition. Management raised FY2026 guidance, and the franchise is exactly the kind of durable, enabling-technology business that compounds over time.

The reason for a HOLD is valuation against incremental risk. At ~\$149 the stock trades near 40x forward adjusted earnings (and an optically extreme ~105x GAAP), a full multiple that already prices in durable growth and a smooth Riverpoint integration — even as that deal adds leverage, dilution, and execution risk. With ~18% upside to the \$175–180 analyst targets in the base case against a bear case ~20–30% lower if integration or the industrial cycle disappoints, the risk/reward is balanced rather than compelling. I'd want a better entry or proof of clean Riverpoint execution to turn more constructive.

## 8. Readability and Clarity Pass

**What Novanta actually does:** Novanta doesn't sell finished products to consumers — it makes the precise, high-tech parts *inside* other companies' machines. If a surgical robot needs a laser that steers with micron accuracy, a DNA sequencer needs a precision optical system, or a factory robot needs exact motion control and machine vision, Novanta supplies those subsystems. About half its business serves medical equipment makers and half serves industrial and semiconductor customers.

**Why the stock is debated:** Novanta is a well-run "compounder" — it grows steadily, generates good cash, and buys other companies to expand (most recently a \$1.45B surgical-consumables business, Riverpoint). Orders are re-accelerating (bookings up 37%). But the stock is expensive (~40x next year's adjusted earnings), and the big acquisition adds debt and issues new shares, so there's more risk if the integration doesn't go smoothly.

**Key terms explained:**

- **OEM component supplier:** Novanta sells parts/subsystems to the companies that build the final equipment, rather than selling the equipment itself.
- **Photonics / precision motion / vision:** Lasers and optics, ultra-precise movement control, and camera-based inspection — Novanta's three core technologies.
- **Automation Enabling Technologies vs Medical Solutions:** Novanta's two reporting segments; roughly half the business each.
- **Book-to-bill:** New orders divided by revenue billed. Above 1.0 (Novanta's 1.10) means the order backlog is growing — a good sign for future revenue.
- **Recurring consumables:** Single-use items (like surgical consumables) that customers reorder repeatedly — steadier and higher-margin than one-time equipment sales.
- **GAAP vs adjusted EPS:** GAAP includes large non-cash acquisition costs (making the P/E look huge); adjusted strips them out and is what management guides on.
