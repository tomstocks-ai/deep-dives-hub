## 1. COMPANY OVERVIEW

**Cerebras Systems Inc.** (NASDAQ: CBRS) is an AI infrastructure company that designs and manufactures the world’s largest AI compute chips — the Wafer-Scale Engine (WSE). Founded in 2015 and headquartered in Sunnyvale, California, Cerebras went public in May 2026 via IPO at \$185 per share, closing its first day at \$311. The company employs approximately 708 people and has built a unique moat around wafer-scale integration, delivering massive compute density for AI training and inference workloads.

### Business Model & Revenue Segments

Cerebras operates a vertically integrated model, designing its own chips and building the CS-3 systems that house them. Revenue is derived from:

| Segment | Description | Revenue Driver |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **AI Compute Systems** | CS-3 systems powered by WSE-3 chips (46,225 mm² die area, 4 trillion transistors) | System sales and leases to hyperscalers and governments |
| **Cloud & Inference Services** | Cerebras Cloud offering on-demand AI compute | Recurring revenue from inference-as-a-service |
| **Software & Support** | Cerebras Software Platform (CSP) and ongoing support contracts | Attach revenue with high margins |

**Key customers** have historically been concentrated: ~86% of 2025 revenue came from UAE-affiliated customers (G42). This concentration is being replaced by two transformative deals: a \$20B+ Master Relationship Agreement with OpenAI for 750MW of AI inference (expandable to 2GW), and an AWS binding term sheet to integrate Cerebras chips into Amazon’s cloud infrastructure.

### Competitive Moat

Cerebras’s moat rests on **wafer-scale integration** — the WSE-3 is the largest chip ever built, avoiding the interconnect bottlenecks that plague multi-chip GPU clusters. A single WSE-3 delivers the compute equivalent of hundreds of NVIDIA GPUs with lower latency and higher memory bandwidth. This is not easily replicated: manufacturing a wafer-scale chip requires custom TSMC partnerships, proprietary packaging, and years of yield optimization. The moat is further deepened by software lock-in — the Cerebras Software Platform optimizes models specifically for WSE architecture, creating high switching costs.

### Management

CEO **Andrew Feldman** co-founded Cerebras in 2015 after leading SeaMicro (acquired by AMD). The leadership team combines deep semiconductor engineering expertise with hyperscaler customer relationships. The board includes veterans from TSMC, AMD, and leading AI research labs. Management has navigated the transition from G42-dependent revenue to a diversified OpenAI + AWS customer base, with CFIUS clearance for the OpenAI deal removing a major regulatory overhang.

## 2. FINANCIAL ANALYSIS

### Income Statement

Cerebras is a high-growth, pre-profitability company with rapidly scaling revenue:

| Metric | 2025 Actual | 2026 Guidance |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Revenue** | \$510M | >\$800M (+50%+) |
| **Revenue Growth** | +76% YoY | +50%+ YoY |
| **Gross Margin** | ~45% (est.) | Pressured by contra-revenue in H1 |
| **EPS (TTM)** | \$0.42 | Negative in Q1 2026 (est. -\$0.14) |
| **P/E (TTM)** | ~559x | N/A (forward losses expected) |

**Quality of earnings:** Revenue growth is genuine but lumpy due to large system sales. The OpenAI deal introduces **contra-revenue** accounting (warrants and performance obligations that reduce reported revenue) and **warrant dilution** that will pressure gross margins starting Q1 2026. Investors should focus on backlog conversion and cash collections rather than pure GAAP revenue in the near term.

**Guidance:** 2026 revenue guidance of >\$800M implies ~50%+ growth. The \$24.6B remaining performance obligation (RPO) backlog provides multi-year visibility, though conversion timing depends on OpenAI ramp and AWS integration milestones.

### Balance Sheet

| Metric | Approximate Value |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Cash & Equivalents** | ~\$1.5B (post-IPO) |
| **Total Debt** | Minimal |
| **Net Debt** | Negative (net cash) |
| **Shares Outstanding** | ~220M (pre-warrant dilution) |
| **Market Cap** | ~\$52B |

The IPO raised substantial capital, leaving Cerebras with a strong net cash position. The primary balance sheet concern is **warrant dilution** from the OpenAI deal — if exercised, these warrants could significantly expand shares outstanding and dilute existing shareholders.

### Cash Flow

Operating cash flow is currently negative as the company scales manufacturing and R&D. Capital intensity is high due to wafer-scale chip fabrication (mask costs, TSMC N3/N2 node commitments, and system assembly). Free cash flow is expected to turn positive in 2027–2028 as the OpenAI deal ramps and unit economics improve with volume.

## 4. GROWTH CATALYSTS

1. **OpenAI Master Relationship Agreement** — The \$20B+ deal for 750MW of AI inference (expandable to 2GW) is the largest in Cerebras history. CFIUS clearance removes regulatory risk. The ramp pace and margin trajectory will be the dominant stock driver in 2026.

2. **AWS Integration** — The binding term sheet with Amazon Web Services to integrate Cerebras chips into AWS infrastructure represents a massive distribution channel. If AWS makes Cerebras available via EC2 or SageMaker, it could unlock thousands of enterprise customers.

3. **Q1 2026 Earnings (June 23)** — First post-IPO report. Expectations: EPS -\$0.14, revenue \$56.65M. Key will be guidance, backlog conversion commentary, and margin outlook.

4. **WSE-3 Volume Ramp** — As TSMC N3/N2 yields improve and manufacturing scales, unit costs should decline, improving gross margins from the H1 2026 trough.

5. **New Customer Wins** — Any announcements beyond OpenAI/AWS (e.g., other hyperscalers, sovereign AI clouds, or government contracts) would validate the TAM and reduce concentration risk.

## 5. RISK FACTORS

### Business Risks

- **Customer Concentration:** Historically ~86% of 2025 revenue came from UAE/G42. While OpenAI + AWS are replacing this concentration, dependency on OpenAI for near-term growth is acute. If OpenAI delays or reduces orders, revenue collapses.
- **Contra-Revenue & Warrant Accounting:** The OpenAI deal includes warrants and performance obligations that reduce reported revenue and margins. This accounting complexity may confuse investors and create headline risk even if underlying demand is strong.
- **Competition:** NVIDIA dominates AI training with GPUs and Grace Hopper superchips. Broadcom (AVGO) and Marvell (MRVL) are winning custom silicon deals at hyperscalers. Cerebras must prove wafer-scale is superior to multi-GPU clusters for inference at scale.
- **Technology Risk:** If the industry shifts to smaller, more efficient chiplets or new architectures (e.g., optical computing, neuromorphic), Cerebras’s wafer-scale bet could become obsolete.

### Financial Risks

- **Valuation Risk:** At ~\$52B market cap on ~\$800M revenue, the stock trades at ~65x sales. Any growth deceleration or margin miss would trigger violent multiple compression.
- **Warrant Dilution:** OpenAI deal warrants could significantly expand shares outstanding, diluting EPS and ownership.
- **Cash Burn:** High R&D and manufacturing capex could exhaust cash faster than expected if revenue ramps delay.

### Macro/Sector Risks

- **AI Capex Cycle:** The entire AI infrastructure trade depends on continued hyperscaler spending. If AI ROI disappoints or macro conditions tighten, CBRS is a high-beta casualty.
- **TSMC Supply Chain:** Cerebras depends on TSMC for wafer-scale fabrication. Any geopolitical disruption to Taiwan or TSMC capacity allocation could halt production.
- **Regulatory Risk:** While CFIUS cleared the OpenAI deal, future foreign customer deals (especially Middle East or China-adjacent) could face scrutiny.

## 7. READABILITY & CLARITY PASS

**What Cerebras actually does:** They make the biggest computer chip in the world — a single chip the size of a dinner plate (literally a full silicon wafer) that can run AI models faster than hundreds of NVIDIA GPUs wired together. They sell these chips in refrigerator-sized systems to companies like OpenAI that need massive AI compute power.

**What 76% revenue growth means at \$510M scale:** Cerebras is adding nearly \$400M in new revenue year-over-year. For a 708-person company, this is extraordinary revenue density. The question is whether it can sustain this pace as the customer base shifts from G42 to OpenAI and AWS.

**What ~65x sales means:** If Cerebras sold \$1 of products last year, investors pay \$65 for a share of the company today. You only pay this if you believe revenue will grow 50%+ annually for several years and margins will expand dramatically. The risk: if growth slows to 30%, the stock could fall 40–50% as the multiple compresses.

**What contra-revenue means:** When Cerebras signed the OpenAI deal, they gave OpenAI warrants (essentially free stock options). Under accounting rules, the value of these warrants reduces the revenue Cerebras can report from the deal. So reported revenue may look weaker than actual cash collections — a key distinction for investors.

## 8. COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

### Peers & Positioning

| Company | Ticker | Focus | Relative Strength vs. CBRS |
|---------|--------|-------|---------------------------|
| **NVIDIA** | NVDA | AI GPUs, networking (Mellanox) | Dominates AI training; CBRS targets inference and specific workloads where wafer-scale latency matters |
| **Broadcom** | AVGO | Custom AI ASICs, networking | Larger scale, deeper resources; CBRS competes on raw compute density and wafer-scale integration |
| **Marvell** | MRVL | Custom AI silicon, networking | Hyperscaler co-design relationships; CBRS is more vertically integrated with its own chip manufacturing |
| **AMD** | AMD | CPUs, GPUs, AI accelerators | Expanding MI300 series; CBRS avoids GPU interconnect bottlenecks with single-wafer architecture |
| **Intel** | INTC | CPUs, AI accelerators (Gaudi) | Struggling in AI; CBRS has taken a niche but high-value position |

**Cerebras’s edge:** Unlike GPU vendors that must stitch together hundreds of chips with complex networking, Cerebras puts the compute on one giant chip. This eliminates chip-to-chip latency, reduces power consumption from interconnects, and simplifies software. The trade-off: lower manufacturing yield (a single defect can ruin the whole wafer) and higher upfront cost. For inference at massive scale — OpenAI’s ChatGPT serving billions of queries — the latency and power advantages can justify the cost.
