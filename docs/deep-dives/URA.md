# URA — Global X Uranium ETF

## 1. OVERVIEW

Global X Uranium ETF (URA) provides exposure to a basket of global uranium mining companies. Top holdings include Cameco, Kazatomprom, Sprott Physical Uranium Trust, NexGen Energy, and Denison Mines. URA is a levered play on uranium spot prices — miners typically amplify uranium moves by 1.5–3x.

**What this means:** URA does not own uranium directly (except via Sprott Physical Uranium Trust in the holdings). It owns shares of companies that explore for, mine, and sell uranium. When uranium prices rise, these stocks typically rise faster.

## 2. FUNDAMENTAL DRIVERS

### The Nuclear Renaissance
Nuclear power is experiencing a global revival. After decades of stagnation, countries are recommitting to nuclear as a baseload, zero-carbon energy source.

- **U.S.:** 93 operating reactors. Advanced reactor programs (Oklo, Kairos, TerraPower) receiving DOE funding. SMR deployment targeted for late 2020s.
- **China:** Building 25+ new reactors. Targeting 150 GW by 2035 (from ~55 GW today).
- **Japan:** 14 reactors restarted post-Fukushima. More restarts planned.
- **Europe:** France expanding nuclear capacity. Several countries reversing phase-out plans.
- **Emerging markets:** India, UAE, Turkey, Bangladesh building first reactors.

**What this means:** Nuclear reactor count is set to grow significantly over the next decade. Each reactor needs uranium fuel, and demand is projected to outstrip supply.

### Supply-Demand Imbalance

- **2025 production:** ~173M lbs U₃O₈
- **2025 demand:** ~204M lbs U₃O₈
- **Deficit:** ~31M lbs — filled by drawing down stockpiles and secondary supplies
- **Projected:** Deficit widens to 50M+ lbs by 2028 as reactor demand grows and mine supply stagnates

**What this means:** The world is consuming more uranium than it produces. Stockpiles are finite. Eventually, prices must rise to incentivize new production.

### Supply Constraints
- **Kazatomprom:** World's largest producer (~40% of global supply). Lowered 2026 production guidance due to operational challenges. Plans 71.5–75.4M lbs — still below state caps.
- **Cameco:** McArthur River mine production reduced. Cigar Lake aging.
- **New projects slow:** Lance, Wheeler River, Honeymoon — all delayed or underfunded.
- **Three-digit incentive price:** Industry consensus says \$100+/lb is needed to justify new greenfield mines given capital costs, timelines, and jurisdictional risk.

### Policy Tailwinds
- U.S. banned Russian uranium imports (2024).
- Uranium reinstated to USGS Critical Minerals List (2025).
- \$2.7B U.S. DOE program to build domestic uranium supply chain.
- EU classifying nuclear as "green" under taxonomy.

## 4. CATALYSTS

- **Utility contracting cycle** — Utilities typically buy 3–5 years forward. The current cycle is just beginning after years of under-contracting.
- **Kazatomprom production shortfalls** — Any further guidance cuts tighten supply immediately.
- **New reactor approvals** — China, U.S., UK, France announcing new builds.
- **Sprott Physical Uranium Trust** — Continues to remove physical uranium from the spot market. AUM ~\$6B.
- **HALEU supply chain** — Advanced reactors need High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium. Only Centrus Energy (LEU) and Orano produce it. Bottleneck creates uranium demand.
- **Japan restarts** — Each restarted reactor adds ~400K lbs/year of demand.

## 5. RISKS

- **Nuclear accident** — Fukushima-style event would freeze reactor development globally.
- **Prolonged recession** — Electricity demand falls; utilities delay refueling.
- **Secondary supply** — Enrichment tails, blended down HEU, and inventory releases could fill gaps temporarily.
- **Political risk** — Kazakhstan political instability; nationalization risk in Africa.
- **Miner dilution** — Junior miners raise capital via equity at low prices, diluting shareholders.
- **Long development timelines** — Even with high prices, new mines take 10+ years.

## 6. SENTIMENT & FLOWS

- **URA AUM:** ~\$2.5B. Saw outflows during the recent correction but stabilizing.
- **Sprott Physical Uranium Trust:** Holdings at record highs. Physical removal from market is a persistent tailwind.
- **Analyst consensus:** Bullish long-term. Citi base case \$100/lb by end of 2026. Sprott sees three-digit prices as necessary for supply response.
- **Short interest:** Low on URA itself, but some junior miners heavily shorted.
- **Institutional:** Increasing allocation to uranium as "energy transition" theme. Pension funds and endowments adding physical exposure via Sprott.

## 7. INVESTMENT THESIS

### Bull Case
Nuclear is the only scalable zero-carbon baseload power. Reactor demand is growing while mine supply is constrained. The deficit is structural and widening. Uranium must reach \$100–150/lb to incentivize new production. URA could double from current levels over 2–3 years.

### Base Case
Steady demand growth meets slowly responding supply. Uranium trades \$90–110. URA grinds back to \$55–65. Annual returns 20–30%.

### Bear Case
Nuclear sentiment sours; recession delays reactor builds; secondary supply fills the gap. Uranium drops to \$60–75. URA falls to \$30–38. Risk is asymmetric — upside is larger than downside given the structural deficit.

## 9. READABILITY PASS

- **ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund):** A fund that trades like a stock. URA holds uranium mining company shares, not uranium metal itself.
- **U₃O₈ (Uranium oxide):** The form of uranium that is traded and used as reactor fuel. Prices are quoted per pound of U₃O₈.
- **HALEU:** High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium — a more concentrated form of fuel needed for advanced reactors. It is harder to produce and creates additional demand for raw uranium.
- **Baseload power:** Electricity that runs 24/7, unlike solar or wind which are intermittent. Nuclear is one of the few carbon-free baseload options.
- **Secondary supply:** Uranium from sources other than mines — stockpiles, recycled weapons material, enrichment byproducts. This can temporarily fill supply gaps.
- **SMR:** Small Modular Reactor — a newer, smaller type of nuclear reactor that can be factory-built and deployed more quickly than traditional large plants.
