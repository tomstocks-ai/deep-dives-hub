# TER — Teradyne, Inc.

Teradyne is the world's leading supplier of automated test equipment (ATE) for semiconductors, and one of the most direct picks-and-shovels beneficiaries of the AI hardware buildout — AI now drives roughly 70% of its sales. Rising test intensity from advanced packaging, HBM/DRAM, and compute SoCs is structurally expanding its market, and demand has exploded: after a modest FY2025 (revenue +13% to \$3.19B), Q1 2026 revenue grew 87% and Q2 is guided to similar growth. The catch is that ATE is still a cyclical business trading at a peak-cycle multiple: even after a ~34% pullback from its 52-week high, at ~\$322 the stock fetches ~60x trailing and ~46x forward earnings on what may be cyclically elevated profits. Real AI tailwind, rich price — a **HOLD**, with clear upside to consensus if the up-cycle proves durable.

## 1. Company Overview

**Teradyne, Inc.** (NASDAQ: TER), founded in 1960 and headquartered in North Reading, Massachusetts, designs and sells automated test systems and industrial robotics. Its equipment verifies that semiconductors and electronic systems work correctly before they ship — a mission-critical step that becomes more complex, and more valuable, as chips get denser and are stacked into advanced packages.

### Business Model and Revenue Segments

| Segment | Description | Q4 2025 Revenue | Role |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|------|
| **Semiconductor Test** | ATE for wafer, package, and system-level test of SoCs, memory (DRAM/HBM), and more | \$883M | Core engine — the AI beneficiary |
| **Product Test** | Test for storage, circuit boards, and defense/aerospace electronics | \$110M | Diversifying, steady |
| **Robotics** | Universal Robots (collaborative robots) and MiR (autonomous mobile robots) — "physical AI" | \$89M | Second growth engine, subscale today |

Semiconductor Test dominates the model. Within it, the AI boom is driving demand across three vectors: **compute** (testing complex AI accelerators and SoCs), **memory** (HBM and DRAM test intensity rising with AI), and **advanced packaging** (chiplets and stacked die require far more test). The Robotics segment (Universal Robots + MiR) targets factory and logistics automation and is Teradyne's bet on "physical AI."

### Competitive Moat

- **Duopoly-like ATE position:** Teradyne and Advantest dominate the high-end SoC and memory test market. Test programs are deeply embedded in customer production flows, creating high switching costs and a long-lived installed base with recurring service revenue.
- **Test-intensity tailwind:** Advanced packaging, chiplets, HBM stacking, and system-level test all raise the amount of test required per device — a structural driver that grows Teradyne's TAM (management frames a path toward a ~\$20B ATE market).
- **Deep customer engineering:** Multi-year co-development with leading chipmakers and OSATs (e.g., the Tokyo Electron KGD collaboration for AI/data-center device screening) locks Teradyne into next-generation roadmaps.
- **Robotics optionality:** Universal Robots and MiR give Teradyne a foothold in industrial automation, a potential second engine if "physical AI" adoption scales.

### Management

Teradyne is led by CEO **Greg Smith**, with a long-tenured engineering-driven leadership team. Management has guided to a \$6B midterm revenue target at 59-61% gross margin, signaling confidence that the AI-driven test-intensity story is structural rather than a one-off spike, while maintaining disciplined capital return via buybacks and a small dividend. (Note: routine insider selling was reported after the stock's large run.)

## 4. Growth Catalysts

1. **AI test intensity (the core driver):** AI accelerators, HBM/DRAM stacks, and advanced packaging require dramatically more test per device. This is the structural engine behind the 87% Q1 2026 revenue jump and management's framing of a path toward a ~\$20B ATE TAM.

2. **Tokyo Electron KGD collaboration:** Teradyne's integrated test-cell solution for known-good-device (KGD) screening of AI and data-center devices, co-developed with Tokyo Electron, targets the advanced-packaging bottleneck where die must be verified before expensive stacking.

3. **\$6B midterm revenue target:** Management's explicit midterm goal (from \$3.19B in FY2025) at 59-61% gross margin, if achieved, would confirm a structurally larger and more profitable business.

4. **Robotics / "physical AI":** Universal Robots and MiR showcased production-ready physical-AI applications at Automate 2026. Scaling this segment would add a second growth engine and improve blended profitability.

5. **Diversification and defense:** Product Test and defense wins (e.g., a \$139.8M Air Force contract for diagnostic test stations) add non-AI ballast, and Nasdaq-100 inclusion (June 2026) broadens the shareholder base.

## 5. Risk Factors

### Business Risks

- **Cyclicality (the defining risk):** ATE demand is historically boom-bust, tied to semiconductor capex and unit cycles. Current earnings may be cyclically elevated, and a digestion phase could cut revenue and profits sharply.
- **Customer concentration:** A handful of large chipmakers and OSATs drive Semiconductor Test demand; order timing is lumpy and any single customer's pause is material.
- **Robotics execution:** Universal Robots and MiR remain subscale and have historically been a margin drag; the "physical AI" thesis is promising but unproven at scale.

### Financial Risks

- **Valuation risk:** At ~60x trailing and ~46x forward earnings, well above the historical 20-25x range, the stock embeds continued strong execution. Multiple compression is the dominant downside driver.
- **Peak-earnings risk:** Paying a premium multiple on potentially peak-cycle earnings compounds downside if the cycle turns.

### Macro and Sector Risks

- **AI capex dependence:** With AI at ~70% of sales, an AI-spending digestion or disappointment would hit Teradyne disproportionately.
- **Semiconductor and export-control cycle:** Broad semi-equipment downturns, trade restrictions, and China exposure can weigh on the addressable market.
- **High beta and prior run:** A ~1.7 beta and a ~250% run off the lows leave the stock exposed to a fast de-rating in any risk-off rotation; recent insider selling has been noted.

## 6. Recommendation

**Rating: <span class="rating-hold">HOLD</span>**

Teradyne is a high-quality franchise at the center of a genuine secular story: the AI hardware buildout is driving a step-change in semiconductor test intensity, and Teradyne — as the leading ATE supplier — is one of the cleanest picks-and-shovels beneficiaries. The 87% revenue growth in Q1 2026, AI at ~70% of sales, the Tokyo Electron KGD collaboration, and a \$6B midterm revenue target all point to a business that may be structurally larger and more profitable than its history suggests.

The reason this is a HOLD rather than a BUY is valuation against cyclicality. At ~\$322 the stock trades near 60x trailing and ~46x forward earnings, far above ATE's historical range, on earnings that may be cyclically elevated. ATE is a boom-bust industry, and paying a peak multiple on peak profits is how investors get hurt when the cycle turns. That said, the risk/reward is more balanced than a typical peak call: the stock has already fallen ~34% from its high, and the ~\$423 consensus implies ~31% upside with street highs far above that. If the AI test-intensity story is truly structural, the bull case is real — but I'd want a better entry or proof of durability first.

**Position Sizing:** 2–3% for investors who want AI-test exposure and can tolerate high volatility and cyclicality. Size for the possibility of a large drawdown if the cycle turns.

**Entry Strategy:** Prefer to accumulate on further weakness — toward the \$250–\$300 zone or on a post-earnings reset — or on confirmation from the July 28 print and 2H guidance that AI test demand is durable rather than peaking. Chasing after a 250% run is poor risk/reward.

## 8. Readability and Clarity Pass

**What Teradyne actually does:** Teradyne makes the machines that test computer chips before they ship. Every advanced chip — especially AI accelerators and the memory stacked next to them — has to be checked to make sure it works, and Teradyne's testers do that checking. As chips get more complex and are stacked into "advanced packages," they need far more testing, which means more Teradyne machines. It also owns two robotics brands (Universal Robots and MiR) that make factory and warehouse robots.

**Why the stock is debated:** The AI boom has sent demand for chip testing soaring — sales jumped 87% in early 2026 and AI is now about 70% of the business. That's genuinely exciting. But chip-equipment demand has always been cyclical (big booms followed by busts), and investors have priced Teradyne at a rich ~46x next year's expected earnings. If AI test demand keeps growing, the stock can grow into that price. If the cycle cools, both earnings and the price could fall hard — which is why it already dropped ~34% from its high.

**Key terms explained:**

- **ATE (automated test equipment):** Machines that verify chips work correctly — Teradyne's core product.
- **Semiconductor Test / Product Test / Robotics:** Teradyne's three segments; Semiconductor Test is by far the largest and the AI beneficiary.
- **HBM / advanced packaging:** High-bandwidth memory and the stacking of multiple chips into one package — both dramatically increase how much testing is needed.
- **KGD (known good device):** Verifying each chip is good *before* it's stacked into an expensive package — the focus of Teradyne's Tokyo Electron collaboration.
- **Cyclicality / peak earnings:** Chip-equipment sales rise and fall in cycles; "peak earnings" means profits may be near a high point that won't last.
- **Physical AI:** Applying AI to robots that act in the real world — Teradyne's robotics angle (Universal Robots, MiR).
