## 1. Company Overview

**Micron Technology, Inc.** (NASDAQ: MU) is one of the "Big Three" global memory semiconductor manufacturers, alongside Samsung and SK hynix. Founded in 1978 and headquartered in Boise, Idaho, Micron designs and produces DRAM, NAND flash, and NOR memory products for data centers, PCs, mobile devices, automotive, and industrial end markets.

**Business Model:**
Micron operates as an integrated device manufacturer (IDM), controlling the entire production chain from wafer fabrication to module assembly. Revenue is driven by memory bit shipments and average selling prices (ASPs), making the company highly sensitive to the cyclical supply-demand dynamics of the memory industry.

**Revenue Segments (FY2025):**
- **Compute and Networking Business Unit (CNBU):** ~45% of revenue — DRAM for servers, PCs, and networking
- **Mobile Business Unit (MBU):** ~20% — LPDRAM and NAND for smartphones
- **Storage Business Unit (SBU):** ~20% — SSDs and NAND for enterprise/cloud
- **Embedded Business Unit (EBU):** ~15% — Automotive, industrial, and IoT memory

**Competitive Moat:**
Micron is the only U.S.-based memory manufacturer with leading-edge fabrication capabilities. Its 1β (1-beta) DRAM and 232-layer NAND technologies are competitive with Samsung and SK hynix. The company is a key supplier of HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) for AI accelerators, with HBM3E products qualified by NVIDIA and other hyperscalers. Geopolitical tailwinds favor domestic memory production given U.S.-China tensions and the CHIPS Act.

**Management:**
CEO Sanjay Mehrotra (co-founder of SanDisk) has led Micron since 2017. Under his leadership, Micron has navigated multiple memory cycles, invested heavily in advanced packaging (HBM), and returned to profitability after deep downturns. The board approved a 30% dividend increase in Q2 2026, reflecting confidence in sustained cash generation.

## 3. Valuation

Micron trades at a premium to historical memory-cycle multiples, but the current cycle is structurally different due to AI-driven HBM demand.

| **Base** | AI memory demand remains strong but moderates, cyclical normalization begins H2 2027 | \$85–90B | \$35–40 | \$1,100–1,300 |
| **Bear** | Memory cycle turns, ASPs collapse, inventory builds, HBM competition intensifies | \$60–70B | \$15–20 | \$600–800 |

**Current Metrics (June 2026):**
- Price: ~\$1,134
- Market Cap: ~\$1.25T
- P/E (TTM): ~49x
- Forward P/E (FY2026): ~28–32x
- P/S: ~14x
- EV/EBITDA: ~25x

**Valuation Commentary:**
At ~49x TTM P/E, Micron is not cheap by historical standards. However, the AI memory cycle is unprecedented — HBM3E carries 3–5x the ASP of standard DRAM and margins are substantially higher. If the company delivers on its \$33.5B Q3 guidance and sustains elevated margins through FY2026, the forward multiple compresses to ~28x, which is reasonable for a company growing revenue 80%+ YoY. The risk is that memory cycles are notoriously violent on the downside.

## 4. Growth Catalysts

1. **AI/HBM Demand Explosion:** HBM is the highest-margin product in Micron's portfolio. NVIDIA's Blackwell and next-gen AI accelerators require HBM3E in massive volumes. Micron is one of only three qualified suppliers (with Samsung and SK hynix).

2. **Data Center Memory Upgrade Cycle:** Beyond HBM, standard DDR5 demand is rising as hyperscalers refresh servers for AI workloads. Cloud memory revenue grew 160%+ in Q2.

3. **CHIPS Act and U.S. Manufacturing:** Micron is investing \$15B+ in domestic fabrication (Boise and New York), supported by CHIPS Act subsidies. This positions the company as the preferred memory supplier for U.S. defense and critical infrastructure.

4. **HBM4 Roadmap:** Micron is developing HBM4 for 2026–2027, which will increase stack height and bandwidth. Early qualification wins could lock in multi-year supply agreements.

5. **Dividend Growth:** The 30% dividend increase signals management confidence in sustained cash generation, making Micron more attractive to income-oriented investors.

## 5. Risk Factors

1. **Memory Cyclicality:** The semiconductor memory market is historically cyclical with boom-bust periods. The current boom is AI-driven, but a slowdown in AI capital spending or inventory digestion could crash ASPs.

2. **Competition:** Samsung and SK hynix are fierce competitors with larger balance sheets. SK hynix currently leads in HBM market share (~50–55%). Samsung is investing aggressively to regain share.

3. **Customer Concentration:** Hyperscalers (NVIDIA, Microsoft, Amazon, Google) represent an outsized share of HBM demand. Any slowdown in their AI infrastructure spending directly impacts Micron.

4. **Capital Intensity:** Memory fabrication requires massive ongoing capex. Micron's Q2 capex was \$5.0B and will remain elevated. If the cycle turns, high fixed costs amplify losses.

5. **Geopolitical Risk:** China is a significant market for memory. U.S. export restrictions and potential Chinese retaliation could impact revenue. Conversely, Micron benefits from being a U.S.-based alternative.

6. **Valuation Risk:** At \$1,134 and 49x TTM P/E, the stock prices in near-flawless execution. Any miss on guidance or margin compression could trigger a sharp correction.

## 6. Recommendation

## 7. Readability Pass

- **HBM (High Bandwidth Memory):** A specialized type of DRAM stacked vertically to deliver extremely high data transfer rates. Used in AI accelerators like NVIDIA GPUs because regular RAM is too slow.
- **ASP (Average Selling Price):** The average price per unit sold. In memory, ASPs rise when demand outstrips supply and fall when the market is oversupplied.
- **IDM (Integrated Device Manufacturer):** A chip company that both designs and manufactures its own chips (unlike fabless companies that outsource manufacturing).
- **1β (1-beta) DRAM:** Micron's most advanced DRAM process node, offering better performance and lower power than prior generations.
- **Capex:** Capital expenditures — money spent on factories, equipment, and facilities.
