# MTZ — MasTec, Inc.

## 1. COMPANY OVERVIEW

MasTec, Inc. is one of the largest infrastructure construction companies in North America. It builds and maintains energy, utility, and communications infrastructure across six segments: Communications, Clean Energy and Infrastructure, Power Delivery, Oil and Gas, Electrical Transmission, and Other.

**Business model:** MasTec is a project-based contractor. It bids on large-scale infrastructure jobs, executes them over multi-year timelines, and recognizes revenue as work progresses. The model is capital-intensive and labor-dependent, with profitability driven by project selection, execution discipline, and backlog quality.

**Revenue segments (FY2025):**
- **Communications:** ~35% — wireless, wireline, and install-to-the-home (broadband/fiber)
- **Clean Energy and Infrastructure:** ~25% — renewables, EV charging, environmental
- **Power Delivery:** ~20% — substations, distribution, grid hardening
- **Oil and Gas:** ~15% — pipeline construction and maintenance
- **Electrical Transmission:** ~5% — high-voltage transmission lines

**Competitive moat:** Scale and relationships. MasTec is a top-3 player in most of its markets. Long-term master service agreements (MSAs) with major utilities, telecoms, and energy companies create recurring revenue visibility. The \$20.3B backlog (Q1 2026) is a record and provides 12+ months of revenue visibility.

**Management:** CEO Jose Mas (son of founder Jorge Mas) has led the company since 2007. The Mas family controls ~20% of shares through Class B voting stock. Insider ownership aligns management with long-term value creation.

**What this means:** MasTec is in a strong growth phase. Revenue grew 32% in FY2025 and accelerated to 34% in Q1 2026. EBITDA margins expanded from 6.3% to 8.0%, showing operating leverage. The company is converting revenue growth into profit growth faster than costs are rising.

### Balance Sheet

- **Cash:** ~\$396M (FY2025)
- **Long-term debt:** ~\$3.0B (total debt ~\$3.0B)
- **Net debt:** ~\$2.6B
- **Backlog:** \$20.3B (Q1 2026) — record high, up 28% YoY

**What this means:** The balance sheet is leveraged but manageable. The \$3.0B debt load is backed by \$20.3B of contracted work. The key risk is that debt covenants could tighten if revenue growth slows or margins compress. The company guided to >\$1B of operating cash flow for FY2026, which would materially deleverage the balance sheet.

### Cash Flow

- **Operating cash flow (FY2025):** ~\$566M
- **Free cash flow:** Lumpy due to working capital swings on large projects
- **FY2026 guidance:** >\$1B operating cash flow

**What this means:** Cash flow is improving but not yet consistent. The FY2026 guidance for >\$1B operating cash flow is a major catalyst — if achieved, it would validate the business model and enable debt paydown.

## 4. GROWTH CATALYSTS

1. **Infrastructure spending supercycle:** Grid modernization, broadband expansion (BEAD program), renewable energy buildout, and data center electrification are all multi-year tailwinds.
2. **Backlog growth:** \$20.3B backlog (Q1 2026) is up 28% YoY. The Clean Energy and Infrastructure segment backlog grew 65%.
3. **Margin expansion:** EBITDA margins improved from 6.3% (FY2024) to 8.0% (FY2025) and are guided to approach double digits. Every 100bps of margin improvement adds ~\$170M of EBITDA.
4. **Cash flow inflection:** FY2026 guidance for >\$1B operating cash flow would validate the model and enable deleveraging.
5. **M&A optionality:** MasTec has a history of acquiring smaller contractors to add capabilities and geographic reach.

## 5. RISK FACTORS

### Business Risks
- **Project execution risk:** Large fixed-price contracts can overrun. One bad project can wipe out a quarter's profit.
- **Customer concentration:** Top-20 customers likely represent a majority of revenue.
- **Labor availability:** Skilled trades (linemen, welders, technicians) are in short supply. Wage inflation is a persistent cost pressure.

### Financial Risks
- **Debt load:** \$3.0B of debt is material. If EBITDA growth stalls, leverage ratios could tighten covenants.
- **Working capital intensity:** Revenue growth requires investment in receivables, inventory, and equipment. Free cash flow can lag earnings.
- **Dilution risk:** If growth capital is needed, equity issuance at high valuations is possible.

### Macro/Sector Risks
- **Interest rates:** Higher rates increase debt service costs and can slow infrastructure spending by utility customers.
- **Tariffs:** Steel, aluminum, and equipment tariffs directly impact material costs on fixed-price contracts.
- **Regulatory/political risk:** Changes in federal infrastructure spending priorities (e.g., BEAD program delays) could impact backlog growth.
- **Economic cyclicality:** Infrastructure spending is less cyclical than residential construction, but not immune to recession.

## 10. READABILITY & CLARITY PASS

**EBITDA** = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Think of it as "cash profits before financing and accounting costs." MasTec's EBITDA margin of 8% means it keeps \$0.08 of cash profit for every \$1 of revenue after paying direct project costs.

**Backlog** = The total value of signed contracts that have not yet been completed. MasTec's \$20.3B backlog is like a pipeline of guaranteed future revenue. At current run rates, this is ~14 months of revenue visibility.

**Master Service Agreement (MSA)** = A long-term contract framework that establishes pricing and terms for future work. MSAs make backlog more predictable because they create recurring relationships rather than one-off bids.

**EV/EBITDA** = Enterprise Value (market cap + debt - cash) divided by EBITDA. It's a valuation ratio that compares the company's total value to its cash profits. MasTec's EV/EBITDA of ~21x on FY2026E is high but not extreme for a fast-growing infrastructure company.

**Fixed-price contract** = A project where the contractor agrees to complete the work for a set price, regardless of cost overruns. This is MasTec's biggest risk — if steel or labor costs rise faster than expected, the company eats the difference.
