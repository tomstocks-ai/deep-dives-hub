## 1. Company Overview

**GE Vernova Inc.** (NYSE: GEV) is a global leader in power equipment, grid infrastructure, and renewable energy solutions. Spun off from General Electric in April 2023, the company operates three core segments: **Power** (gas turbines and services), **Wind** (onshore and offshore), and **Electrification** (grid solutions, power conversion, solar, and storage). Headquartered in Cambridge, MA, and led by CEO Scott Strazik, GE Vernova is uniquely positioned as the "picks and shovels" provider for the global energy transition, AI data center power infrastructure, and grid modernization.

## 2. Investment Thesis

### 2.1 The AI Power Infrastructure Supercycle

Data center power demand is projected to grow at a 15–20% CAGR through 2030, driven by AI training and inference workloads. GE Vernova's gas turbines, grid solutions, and power conversion equipment are critical inputs for hyperscalers building multi-gigawatt campuses. Unlike pure-play data center operators, GEV captures value across the entire power delivery chain—from generation to transmission to distribution.

### 2.2 Record Backlog Provides Multi-Year Visibility

Total backlog reached **\$163B** as of Q1 2026, with equipment backlog surging **67% YoY** to \$76B and services backlog growing **12% YoY** to \$87B. A Q1 book-to-bill of **~2.0x** implies revenue acceleration ahead. The company raised FY2026 guidance after the quarter, signaling management confidence in execution.

### 2.3 Margin Expansion Story

Q1 2026 adjusted EBITDA jumped **87% YoY** to \$896M, with margin expanding **390 bps**. Operating leverage from scale, mix shift toward higher-margin services, and disciplined cost management are driving profitability faster than revenue. This dynamic is expected to continue as the backlog converts.

### 2.4 Energy Transition Tailwinds

Grid modernization mandates in the U.S. and Europe, renewable energy buildouts, and the electrification of transport and industry create sustained demand for GEV's electrification segment. Offshore wind—while lumpy—represents a long-term growth vector as countries commit to decarbonization targets.

## 4. Business Segments

### 4.1 Power

The Power segment manufactures and services gas turbines, steam turbines, and generators for utilities and industrial customers. It is the largest revenue contributor and a primary beneficiary of AI data center power demand. Gas turbine orders have accelerated as hyperscalers seek reliable, dispatchable power for their campuses.

### 4.2 Wind

Wind includes onshore and offshore turbine manufacturing, installation, and services. While the onshore market is competitive, offshore wind represents a higher-margin, longer-cycle growth opportunity. GEV's Haliade-X offshore platform is one of the most powerful turbines in the market, with strong backlog in Europe and emerging U.S. projects.

### 4.3 Electrification

Electrification encompasses grid solutions (substations, transformers, switchgear), power conversion (inverters, drives), and solar/storage integration. This segment is the most directly exposed to grid modernization and renewable energy interconnection trends. It is also the fastest-growing segment, with significant order momentum from data center and utility customers.

## 6. Risks & Concerns

### 6.1 Execution Risk on Backlog

A \$163B backlog is only valuable if it converts to revenue and profit on schedule. Supply chain constraints, labor shortages, or project delays could push out revenue recognition and strain working capital.

### 6.2 Wind Segment Volatility

Offshore wind has faced headwinds globally, including permitting delays, rising costs, and policy uncertainty. While GEV's offshore platform is technologically advanced, the segment may experience lumpy orders and margin pressure in the near term.

### 6.3 Valuation Sensitivity

At ~\$1,110, GEV trades at a premium multiple. Any slowdown in AI data center spending, grid investment, or energy transition momentum could compress the multiple quickly. The stock is also up significantly from its 52-week low of \$486.96, creating potential for profit-taking volatility.

### 6.4 Geopolitical & Regulatory Risk

A significant portion of GEV's backlog is international. Tariffs, export controls, or changes in foreign energy policy could impact project economics and order flow. Domestic content requirements for grid infrastructure could also affect cost structures.

### 6.5 Services Renewal Risk

Services backlog, while recurring in nature, depends on customers continuing to operate GEV equipment. Early retirements of gas turbines (due to policy or economics) could reduce the long-term services opportunity.
