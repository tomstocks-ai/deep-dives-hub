# LEU — Centrus Energy Corp.

## 1. COMPANY OVERVIEW

Centrus Energy Corp. (NYSE: LEU) is the only U.S.-owned uranium enrichment company. It supplies nuclear fuel components for commercial and government reactors worldwide.

**Business Segments:**

- **LEU Segment (~58% of revenue):** Sells separative work units (SWU), natural uranium hexafluoride, and enriched uranium products to nuclear utilities.
- **Technical Solutions (~42% of revenue):** Provides engineering, manufacturing, and technical services to government and private-sector customers, including the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE).

**Competitive Moat:**

- Only U.S.-licensed HALEU (high-assay low-enriched uranium) production facility — a critical national security asset.
- \$3.9B contracted backlog provides multi-year revenue visibility.
- \$900M DOE investment to expand domestic enrichment capacity.
- 14 major suppliers across 13 states create a domestic supply chain that is hard to replicate.

**Management:** CEO Daniel Poneman has deep government and energy policy experience. Insider ownership is meaningful but not dominant.

## 4. GROWTH CATALYSTS

1. **HALEU Monopoly:** Centrus is the only U.S.-licensed HALEU producer. Demand is growing for next-generation reactors (SMRs, advanced reactors) that require HALEU.

2. **DOE Partnership:** \$900M DOE investment plus potential billions more for domestic nuclear fuel supply chain. Centrus completed a 900-kg HALEU delivery milestone in 2024.

3. **Oklo Joint Venture:** Exploring deconversion services for HALEU with Oklo — ties into the fast-growing SMR ecosystem.

4. **Backlog Growth:** \$3.9B contracted backlog provides visibility through 2028+. New utility contracts are being signed as countries seek non-Russian enrichment.

5. **Expansion Capex:** Multi-billion-dollar investment planned for Oak Ridge, Tennessee facility. If executed, capacity could 3–5x over the next decade.

6. **Import Waivers:** U.S. government import waivers for 2026–2027 deliveries expand cash flexibility.

## 5. RISK FACTORS

### Business Risks
- **Customer Concentration:** Nuclear utilities are a small, concentrated customer base. Loss of a major utility contract would hurt.
- **Execution Risk:** Multi-billion-dollar expansion is capital-intensive and complex. Cost overruns or delays are possible.
- **Technology Risk:** Centrifuge technology is mature but competing enrichment technologies (laser enrichment) could disrupt.

### Financial Risks
- **Lumpy Revenue:** Contract timing creates quarterly volatility. Q1 2026 earnings "missed" expectations despite raised guidance.
- **Capital Intensity:** Free cash flow is negative during expansion. The company must balance growth spending with balance sheet strength.
- **Short Interest:** 21.58% of shares outstanding are sold short (4.25M shares). High short interest amplifies volatility.

### Macro/Sector Risks
- **Uranium Price Sensitivity:** SWU pricing is tied to uranium markets. A sustained drop in uranium prices would compress margins.
- **Regulatory Risk:** NRC/DOE policy shifts could impact HALEU demand or funding.
- **Geopolitical:** The push for domestic enrichment is partly driven by desire to reduce reliance on Russian supply. A geopolitical thaw could reduce urgency.
- **ESG/Nuclear Sentiment:** Public opposition to nuclear power, though fading, remains a tail risk.

## 10. READABILITY PASS

**What Centrus Actually Does:** They run centrifuges that spin uranium gas to increase its purity. Utilities buy this enriched uranium to fuel nuclear power plants. The "SWU" they sell is basically the work of spinning — the higher the purity, the more work (and revenue).

**HALEU Explained:** Most reactors use uranium enriched to 5%. Advanced reactors need 19.9% — this is HALEU. Higher enrichment = more energy from less fuel. Centrus is the only U.S. company licensed to make it.

**Backlog Means:** \$3.9B in signed contracts. They have already promised to deliver fuel and been paid or will be paid. This is not "maybe" revenue — it is contractual.

**Short Interest at 21%:** More than 1 in 5 shares are borrowed and sold by traders betting the price will fall. If good news hits, these traders must buy shares to cover — potentially driving the price up fast (a "short squeeze").

**Beta of 1.34:** If the S\&P 500 moves 1%, LEU typically moves 1.34%. This cuts both ways — more upside in bull markets, more downside in bear markets.
