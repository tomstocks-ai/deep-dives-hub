## 1. Executive Summary

RTX Corporation (formerly Raytheon Technologies Corporation, renamed July 2023) is one of the world's largest aerospace and defense conglomerates. Formed from the 2020 merger of Raytheon Company and United Technologies Corporation, RTX operates through three primary segments: **Collins Aerospace** (avionics, interiors, mission systems), **Pratt & Whitney** (commercial and military jet engines), and **Raytheon** (missiles, defense systems, intelligence, and space). The company serves as a top-tier U.S. defense prime contractor with significant exposure to missile defense, hypersonics, NATO replenishment, and the ongoing commercial aerospace recovery.

The investment thesis centers on sustained global defense spending growth, a record backlog exceeding \$200B, margin expansion from operational restructuring, and a multi-year commercial aerospace upcycle driving aftermarket demand at Pratt & Whitney and Collins Aerospace. FY2025 full-year EPS of \$5.02 (up from \$3.58 in 2024) and Q4 2025 sales of \$24.2B (up 12% YoY, up 14% organically) demonstrate accelerating execution. The 12-month EPS as of May 2026 stands at \$5.41, reflecting continued earnings momentum.

## 2. Business Model & Revenue Streams

### 2.1 Collins Aerospace
Collins Aerospace is a global leader in aerospace and defense products, providing avionics, flight decks, cabin interiors, mission systems, and power and control systems. Revenue is driven by both OEM deliveries (tied to commercial aircraft production rates at Boeing and Airbus) and a large, growing aftermarket business supported by an installed base of over 100,000 aircraft. Defense sales include communications, electronic warfare, and integrated mission systems for military platforms.

### 2.2 Pratt & Whitney
Pratt & Whitney designs, manufactures, and services aircraft engines for commercial, military, and business aviation. The Geared Turbofan (GTF) engine family powers the Airbus A320neo family and is central to the commercial recovery narrative. Military engines include the F135 for the F-35 Lightning II and the F119 for the F-22 Raptor. A multi-year fleet management and aftermarket services model generates recurring revenue and high-margin spare parts sales.

### 2.3 Raytheon
Raytheon is the defense systems and intelligence segment, encompassing missile defense (Patriot, THAAD, NASAMS), precision strike (Tomahawk, JASSM, hypersonics), naval and land systems, and intelligence & space solutions. This segment is the primary beneficiary of elevated U.S. and allied defense budgets, with long-cycle production contracts providing multi-year revenue visibility.

### 2.4 Revenue Mix
- **Defense:** ~55-60% of total revenue (Raytheon + defense portions of Collins and Pratt)
- **Commercial Aerospace:** ~40-45% (Collins OEM and aftermarket, Pratt commercial engines and services)
- **Geographic:** ~70% U.S., ~30% international (including NATO and allied nations)

## 3. Competitive Landscape & Moat

RTX competes across multiple domains:
- **Defense Primes:** Lockheed Martin (LMT), Northrop Grumman (NOC), General Dynamics (GD), Boeing (BA)
- **Commercial Aerospace:** Safran (via CFM joint venture competitor), Honeywell Aerospace, GE Aerospace
- **Engine OEMs:** GE Aerospace (CFM LEAP competitor to GTF), Rolls-Royce

**Competitive Moat:**
- **Scale and Integration:** Few competitors match the breadth of avionics, propulsion, and defense systems under one roof.
- **Backlog Depth:** Record backlog exceeding \$200B provides years of revenue visibility and pricing power.
- **Regulatory and Certification Barriers:** Aircraft engines and major avionics systems require multi-year certification processes, creating high switching costs.
- **Aftermarket Lock-In:** Long-term service agreements and proprietary parts create recurring, high-margin revenue streams difficult for competitors to displace.
- **Classified and Strategic Programs:** Participation in hypersonics, missile defense, and nuclear command-and-control systems involves security clearances and institutional knowledge that cannot be easily replicated.

### 4.3 Segment Margins

- **Pratt & Whitney:** Margins recovering as GTF fleet matures and inspection/campaign costs normalize.
- **Raytheon:** High-teens operating margins, supported by cost-plus and fixed-price production contracts with favorable escalation clauses.

## 6. Bull, Base & Bear Cases

### Bull Case — Target: \$220–\$230
- Accelerated U.S. and allied defense budgets (NDAA above 4% real growth) drive record bookings at Raytheon.
- Pratt & Whitney GTF aftermarket ramps faster than expected, with inspection campaigns completed ahead of schedule and margin recovery to pre-pandemic levels.
- Collins Aerospace benefits from Boeing production recovery and Airbus rate hikes, with aftermarket margins expanding to 20%+.
- Successful M&A or portfolio optimization (e.g., divestiture of lower-margin non-core assets) unlocks shareholder value.
- Multiple expansion to 20x+ forward EPS as investors re-rate defense primes for growth and stability.

### Base Case — Target: \$195–\$205
- Steady execution across all three segments with mid-single-digit organic revenue growth.
- Defense budgets grow at inflation-plus, maintaining backlog above \$200B.
- Commercial aerospace recovery continues at a measured pace; Pratt margins normalize but do not surge.
- Share buybacks and modest dividend growth support EPS growth of 8-10% annually.
- Stock trades at 18-19x forward earnings, consistent with historical large-cap defense/aerospace multiples.

### Bear Case — Target: \$160–\$170
- U.S. defense budget cuts or program delays (e.g., F-35, Next Generation Air Dominance) reduce Raytheon bookings.
- Commercial aerospace slowdown — Boeing production issues or Airbus order cancellations pressure Collins OEM and Pratt deliveries.
- Pratt & Whitney faces unexpected GTF technical issues or prolonged shop visit costs, delaying margin recovery.
- Margin compression from fixed-price contract overruns or supply chain inflation not fully recovered through escalation.
- Multiple compression to 15x forward EPS on earnings stagnation or decline.

## 7. Key Risks

1. **Defense Budget Volatility:** RTX's largest revenue source is U.S. government contracts. Shifts in geopolitical priorities, deficit reduction efforts, or administration changes can alter funding levels for missile defense, hypersonics, and naval programs.

2. **Commercial Aerospace Cyclicality:** Collins and Pratt are exposed to commercial aircraft build rates. A recession, airline financial stress, or further Boeing production delays could reduce OEM demand and delay aftermarket recovery.

3. **Program Execution Risk:** Large fixed-price development contracts (e.g., Next Generation Interceptor, hypersonics) carry cost overrun risk. Technical failures or schedule slips can result in margin erosion and contract penalties.

4. **Regulatory and Trade Risk:** Export controls (ITAR/EAR) restrict international sales. Changes in trade policy or sanctions can limit access to key allied markets. Additionally, Pratt & Whitney faces environmental and noise regulations affecting engine certification.

5. **Integration and Restructuring Risk:** Although the Raytheon-UTC merger is largely complete, ongoing operational restructuring (e.g., supply chain consolidation, facility rationalization) could disrupt execution or incur higher-than-expected costs.

## 8. Management & Governance

**CEO Christopher T. Calio** took the helm in 2024, bringing deep aerospace and defense operating experience. He previously led Pratt & Whitney and has emphasized operational excellence, margin expansion, and capital discipline. The management team has a strong track record of delivering merger synergies (exceeding original \$1.5B target) and returning cash to shareholders.

**Governance:** Board includes former government and industry leaders with relevant defense and aerospace expertise. Compensation is tied to EPS growth, cash flow, and relative total shareholder return. ESG initiatives focus on sustainable aviation (SAF compatibility, engine efficiency) and defense ethics.
